What is a religious kitsch?

“Art or objets d'art characterized by worthless pretentiousness…” is the definition of “kitsch” in Oxford English Dictionary Second Edition on CD-ROM (v. 4.0) © Oxford University Press 2009.

Many of the great works of art in Western culture are of a religious nature, such as the Last Supper of Leonardo da Vinci. This painting is an iconic work of art, and no one would say that it is kitsch. However, there are plenty of kitschy reproductions of it, such as those printed on large velvet panels or molded in inexpensive plastic in 3d.

Kitsch frequently occurs when artistic depictions of Jesus, Mary, the saints, and other religious art becomes mixed with excessive sentimentality. Care must be taken to avoid insulting or otherwise hurting the sensibilities of those of a simple faith who may find such depictions meaningful. The solution here is not criticism or condemnation, which may actually be thinly-veiled elitism. Helping people to deepen their faith, and exposing them to religious art of a less sentimental nature is a more constructive approach.

Kitsch may also refer, as other answers have suggested, to cheap, mass produced religious items deserving of greater nobility and quality. The best response to these is, don’t buy them.

Plastic saints, Jesus or Mary statuettes for your dashboard, tiny bottles of holy water from the Church of the Sepulcher in Jerusalem, tourist trinkets at Lourdes or Bethlehem, actually any religious material that is produced for mass consumption. A rosary or crucifix can be a work of art and have great value, but not if there are a hundred of them in plastic hanging from a hook for sale in a shop.

I’ve listed Christian kitsch, but I’ve seen the equivalent for Islam in Morocco and Cairo. For Judaism, tiny mezuzahs might fall under this category, too. The distinguishing factor is that it’s mass produced for profit and not for piety (although the purchaser may be pious).
